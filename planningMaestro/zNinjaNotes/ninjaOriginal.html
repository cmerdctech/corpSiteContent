These notes are the original design ideas for the Planning Ninja, descendant of The Planning Discipline and precursor to the Planning Maestro.
Ninja Intro

Goal Oriented Management


Here's where we start: You are already doing a good job. Nothing's perfect but, your colleagues are taking care of their business. Your customers and other stakeholders are engaged and productive. Nothing about this is about taking you from sucks to good. If you feel like you are already behind the eight ball, broke, unsuccessful and feeling confused, we are not talking about you (sorry).


The problem we are trying to solve is how to deal with the fact that you have lots of ideas, that you have goals, that you think that you and your colleagues can do more and then you need to keep track of whatever you're doing as you go from abstract goal to getting something done. Worse, these ideas come and go so quickly that it's maddening.


Let's pause a moment. Most really inventive people carry notebooks in which they write down every single idea. They know that most of those ideas are absolutely worthless but, they also know that you can't tell which are good until you look back on them.


So, the first problem to solve is getting into the habit of recording your goals and ideas as they occur. You realize that only some will be acted upon but you want them tee'd up, ready to go, stored in a place where the distance between writing it down and getting it going is zero.


When you get to that idea, you want to be able to keep track of your noodling around. Maybe you want to make a Ben Franklin list, pros/cons. Maybe you want to look at it as technical or marketing advantages with more complicated properties. Perhaps a favorite ROI spreadsheet to do the arithmetic about something important to your creative production.


You might later want to ask a colleague or stakeholder for help. You might have gotten to the place where you have identified an important goal. If it requires collaboration, and everything does, then you can share the goal with them. You'll probably have talked on the phone, had lunch or saw them in the hallway but you want a place to compare notes online.


Of course, in the view of a goal oriented manager, there is no hierarchy, only collaboration. That means each of the people sharing a goal has an entire management space that does the same things as another management space. For that goal, every derived management space has a mission, vision, or whatever you want as the properties and methods available to that goal. Everyone that is shared into a goal sees the common management space, or not, depending on how it works. People can share as, for example, a supervising stakeholder who only sees the formal status view (whatever that means to the people who see the edit formal status view). 


Planning Ninja



I'm leaning toward sticking with The Planning Discipline. Instead of allowing this to be seen as an uninspired adherence to principle, imagine it as ninja stuff. Black ninja suits. 


No. Not literally. But going with a cool, slightly aggressive tone. We're not people who are like everyone else. We are people that do stuff. We establish goals and collaborate to achieve them. We use the Achievement Manager to exchange ideas. One of the things about us is that we want to be sure that we are actively communicating with each member of a goal in ways that help you all coordinate properly. 


One way to do that is with the (soon to be renamed) Metrics tool. It allows you to add specifics to the idea. Those specifics are intended to be things that require action that affects others, including the outside world. A ninja would become more certain of the correctness of the goal (in likelihood of success, completeness or something else important to the creative process. One way to do that discipline oneself to always write down a brief description of what it looks like if the goal is achieved. Perhaps also ask a question to yourself (and the others who share that goal with you) in the future, eg, "Do we still think that we can accomplish this goal according to plan?"


But, here's another thing a Planning Discipline Ninja (and I remind you, I'm not planning a literal ninja) would do: Communicate with incredible sophistication. There are a lot of things that a Planning Ninja would do that require communicating to achieve the support of the stakeholder community. Things one does include financial reporting, schedule reporting, resource allocation, etc. Everything can be turned into a presentation page. Every presentation page can have a variety of extra stuff, especially comments and links to other presentation (or any other kind) pages.


A goal would have the ability to seen in overview with a dash board that allows the management of the guest roles, ie, the public facing website. In addition to the obvious template stuff, basically any element in the entire Management Space can be listened to by a guest role page. Of course, the public facing website would provide a feed to other tools, as well as HTML for humans. All of it subject to cool role-based management with an underlying publishing system to allow editorial control by the members of the goal.



The thing is, your main goal is to empower yourself and the people around you to achieve goals. Nobody can control another person like a productivity puppet. Every activity, in the end, should be a creative activity. You don't want to limit collaborators' ideas by assertions about their process. You don't want other peoples' details in your head. You want to collaborate on creating and developing goals that can be satisfied according to some criterion.




One of the ideas of martial arts is to use the opponents wait or force against you letting him push at the last minute diving in pulling him along the path he was going to go anyway


The planning Manager allows you to bring people along based on the force of their enthusiasm and engagement. It helps draw out the creativity out of people by treating all business interaction as collaboration. By encouraging people to interact in terms of goals, the achievement manager naturally encourages people to develop multiple agreements about the pursuit of that goal.


By treating management feedback as a goal, people tend to see it as something to get right both in the authoring of the goal (thus increasing by in) and description of success  not a burden. 


With buy in and the enthusiasm it engenders, it is reasonable to think that you have created a decent foundation for the fulfillment of a goal in a way that will be reasonably satisfying for all involved. 


By including all stakeholders in the management space as collaborators, the achievement manager treats their interaction as an exchange of goals,too. It also includes the normal security idea as expressed in the roles and their use in the system. 


For good measure, stakeholders can also take on any other rules, it is in fact incorrect to make a distinction between stakeholder and collaborator except for the emotional state of the user. That's beyond the scope of this project. 


Stakeholders can, consequently, slip among roles flexibly. You never stop being a stakeholder. To be a user, you are a stakeholder (though you don't need to be a user to be a stakeholder).

Planning Ninja

▾ Planning Ninja

• An elaborate note-keeping tool for people who want to get stuff done

• Based on the idea of shared goals and buy-in

• Easy to use, comprehensive, conceptually solid

▾ The Opposite of Micro-Management

• Goals are not assignments. Not a list of tasks.

• A goal is an opportunity for collaborators to join the effort and apply their creative energy to accomplishment

• For collaborators, a goal is a new idea that needs to be explored.

• Each collaborator is a Planning Ninja, too

▾ The Achievement Manager

• A tool designed by Planning Ninjas, for Planning Ninjas

• Goals are identified and shared; collaborators buy-in

• Every goal is supported by a Management Space shared by collaborators

• Collaborators make new goals and share them with new collaborators

▾ Ideas are a Dime a Dozen

• They flit out of existence as soon as they pass

• They only exist when observed (Calling Mr Schroedinger!)

• That piece of napkin will be at the bottom of a pile, lost forever, in two days

• But if you text the words on it or a picture of it to your Planning Ninja Management Space…

▾ The Distance between Idea and Action is a Goal

• Maybe the idea is a goal; maybe it inspires ten

• A goal is a expression of what success will look like

• And a Management Space where collaborators work together to find ways to make it happen

• Accountable actions, finance and timelines

▾ Visibility Keeps the Plan Alive

• Progress information maintains enthusiasm and buy-in

• Controlled web visibility provides information for stakeholders, collaborators and the public

• Live information web presentation tools

• PDF generator puts any part, or all, of a plan on paper

▾ Customize for the Way You Work

• Definable user roles and permissions

• Customize menus, access to tools

• Control access to information

• Word mapping to express the user interface and everything in the language of your business

▾ Progressive Improvement

• Create new plans based on old plans

• Pick and choose the parts that are still needed

• Move forward budget, actuals… Or not

▾ Get Started Today

• Free startup account. Full access for one month. No credit card required.

• Data stays online for a year, even if you never pay

• Starts at $15/month ($125/year). Details

▾ Doing What You Do

• Do a Ben Franklin, crank up a little spreadsheet

• Take a few notes, ask a few questions

• Figure out if an idea is worth pursuing

• In a place where they will be easy to find

▾ Comprehensive Tool to Keep Track of Accomplishment

• Explain how you will know if a goal is successfully fulfilled

• Identify accountable activities (who, when, money, success)

• Review collaboration and results in the context of goals.

• Provide visibility to collaborators, stockholders and the public

• Support ongoing efforts by duplicating and editing plans to make new plans

Obviously, bugs will be highlighted. So that these notes can be found, I will use the same title for all the email messages.

Bug #1: I cannot find a way to send a link to my vision statement.


For me, being able to show my work is very important. I thought that creating a presentation is the way to do that. At reporting->presentations, I found an opportunity to rename my Vision. I revised it to say “The Future” and it shows that in the reorder list.


However, when I created a report and put Vision on it, the title did not appear.


Bug #2: The new name of the Vision does not appear on the report.


When I saw that, I realized that the site name is wrong. My intention has changed. I now want it to refer to the Achievement Manager in a romantic way. I went to admin->sites and clicked on the edit button. It won’t let me rename the site.


Bug #3: There is apparently no way to change the name of the site.


Then I thought, I know, I will send a link to the presentation that displays Vision but shows the words I wrote for The Future. I grabbed the URL and tried it in a different browser. It won’t show it to me because I’m not logged in there.


Feature Request: A presentation (and probably everything else, too) should have a check box that allows it to be viewed by people who are not logged in.


Feature Request: It would be very nice to be able to create as short URL alias for URLs in the site.





Working on my internal analysis, strengths inevitably suggested weaknesses. I wanted to work on both at once but the tool would not let me.

 

Feature Request: Tools should not limit the user to only one active editor per page. Users often want to edit several related things at once. We do not want them to have to copy their strengths/weaknesses into a word processor to get them just so. (As the day wore on, this became really annoying to me.)

 

One of my weaknesses, I realized, was more correctly specified as a point of External Analysis. When I went to that tool, I found that the category (marketing) was not there. Turns out I can edit these categories. Several are not relevant to my organization. I set to delete one an got this dialog asking if I want to delete Educational Trends.

 

I have entered no data for this topic. I left it there because I was afraid I would ruin other websites.

 

Feature Request: Don’t show the warning if there is no data for that External Category. Alternately, add text to the warning that makes the scope clear, ie, that deleting the category won’t harm others. (Of course, if it *would* harm others, that’s a bug, a bad bug.)

 

Then I realized that there are many more categories in the external list editor than shows on my external analysis page. I was perplexed that I couldn’t see any way to control the visibility of the errant category. Eventually, I thought to edit it. 

 

Feature Request: Move Active/Inactive to a separate tool, eg “Edit Delete Visibility”.

 

Feature Request: Add an indicator of the current visibility status. I eventually had to write down the ones I wanted to get rid of because I got so confused having to click through to find out how it was set in the editor. Eg, “Edit Delete Visibility(visible)”

 

Feature Request: Make the external categories much smaller and more universal.

 

Then I wondered about district vs site category. I have literally no clue about the difference or what either of those words mean in this context. I tried it out. When I chose ‘site category’, it disappeared from my External Analysis editor even though it was set to ‘active’.

 

Feature Request: Do something to make the district/site distinction understandable. I can’t suggest what because I don’t have a clue what it means.

 

Later, I spent awhile thinking about something else. When I came back, the site had logged me out. I had to log back in and try to remember where I left off.

 

Feature Request: Allow the user to turn off the logout function. This is not a banking website. 

 

Feature Request: Remember where I was and take me back there when I return (this probably requires implementation of the ‘bookmarkable’ request from yesterday).

 

I focused on Competition Analysis. I would name something that someone might use instead of the Achievement Manager and the google for awhile to figure out whether I was right. I wished I had a place to store notes and URLs.

 

Feature Request: Allow the attachment of arbitrary meta-data (comments, files, etc) to any entry. Provide controls to allow the user to specify when it is seen.

 

Then I looked at the next menu item, Needs. Looking for guidance (the guidance has been very helpful), it just says “to be defined”.

 

Bug Report: The explanatory text on the Needs page says “to be defined” and should say something more helpful.

 

Feature Request: Upgrade the explanatory text to refer to additional information. This might include a user generated forum discussing that topic and/or one that is specific to that customer and it’s stakeholders.

 

Winding down for the day, I took a look at the remaining menu selections. I note a couple of things…

 

1) All of the planning pages look pretty much the same. They present a box to type a list of items. Once items are entered, some of them have complex substructures (Objectives, Strategies, Competition Analysis) that is a complete surprise to the user. We need to find a way to let the person know what to expect when these are created both so that they can know what to enter and what additional information is needed. Also, to let them know where certain kinds of information belong.

 

2) We need to strip out the school jargon. District, Planning, etc are sprinkled through out. This occurs in places where it probably can be turned off but also in places where it can’t, eg, the site/district confusion I mentioned for External Categories. It is very disconcerting to me as a non-education user. 

 

And finally...

 

Feature Request: I want to have a single button that will show (then hide) all of the information on the page with each element expanded to show all of its subordinate elements. This should also allow me to show or hide the editing controls so I can more easily read the information to see if it’s balanced and complete. 

 

But don’t get me wrong. I think I am going to love this tool. It is closer to “a place for everything and everything in its place” than any tool I have ever used and that includes a few I have written myself.

Wait!! I just discovered that the association of goals is only with actions. In my view, the goal is the root of all things.
